The attack of the Vikings by the river and the response led by Heude the Count of Paris

In the fall of 885, Paris is besieged by the Vikings. During the fight, a man is more courageous: Heude comte de Paris. He organizes the response of the invasion from the tower of the Châtelet. Boiling water is thrown at the assailants and a shower of fiery arrows.

The dark memory of the Place du Châtelet

At the beginning of the 9th century, the city of Paris was bruised by misfortunes. The tower of Châtelet crystallizes them. Initially a defense tower, the Châtelet is transformed into a sinister prison. Throughout the Middle Ages until the Revolution, prisoners are locked up and sometimes tortured in it. Their screams mingled with those of the slaughtered beasts. The tower of the Châtelet was demolished in 1804 to repeal all the sad memories related to this prison.

Oppositions between Catholics and Protestants, a time of obscurantism and religious intolerance where street killings are On Sunday, August 24, 1572, during the reign of Charles IX, tension appeared between Catholics and Protestants. Catherine de Medici, the mother of Charles IX believes that one of her son’s advisers, who were Protestant, has a bad influence on him. Jealous, she plans her assassination but it ends in failure. For fear of reprisals, she convinces the king to eliminate all Protestants. Thus in the night of August 24 to August 25, 1572, the Catholic people massacre Protestants. In the context of the wars of religion, this night marks the climax of religious intolerance.

The will of Philippe Auguste to clean up Paris

The twelfth century is marked by the reign of Philip Augustus, a king who had the will to clean up Paris including the cemetery Innocents. This cemetery was insalubrity itself. Pigs were there as well as prostitutes. The pigs were sent back to the farm and a great clean up was made to restore the place to its atmosphere of contemplation. A wall was built and doors installed. Since then, you can only enter the cemetery during the day and not at night because the doors are closed.

A singular fight between Yves and the giant

Paris is coveted by Otto II, the Germanic Emperor. In October 978, he tried to invade the city, then ruled by Count Hugues Capet. A giant Germanic soldier is sent to Paris to insult the Parisian people daily. Otton II hopes by this provocation to engage the fight. Hugues Capet, who no longer supports this provocation, hires a knight named Yves to defend the honor of his people. This is how a singular fight between Yves and the giant begins. Yves wins the victory.
